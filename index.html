01

Buoyancy & Body Position

The foundation of every AquaCats method

1
Buoyancy & Body Position
The single most important thing you will teach · Everything else depends on this
The Most Important Thing to Understand Have you ever watched someone struggling to swim? They are almost always vertical — jolting up and down, splashing their arms, fighting the water. That vertical posture creates resistance, causes sinking, and makes swimming feel impossible. AquaCats methods are built entirely around fixing this. We shift the swimmer from vertical to horizontal. That one change transforms everything.
What "Horizontal" Actually Means The goal is a swimmer aligned head to toe along the waterline. Specifically: face looking down into the water, hips up at the surface, knees straight, feet kicking. One long flat line from head to feet — nothing breaking that line. This is the position every AquaCats method is designed to create and protect.

A question we get often: "How do you teach babies these things when they can't understand you?" The answer is that we are not teaching their mind — we are training their body. Through repetition and correct physical support, the swimmer's body learns to naturally adjust to the correct position. The muscle memory develops before the understanding does. That is the method.

What Is Buoyancy?

  • →Buoyancy is the natural force that pushes the body upward in water. Swimmers do not need to "try" to float — the water does the work automatically.
  • →When the body is horizontal, buoyancy lifts the swimmer toward the surface, floating becomes easier, and forward movement requires less effort.
  • →Children under 1 are the most naturally buoyant they will ever be. As they age, this natural buoyancy decreases. This is why we recommend starting lessons as early as 3 months — the earlier we begin, the more we can work with the body's natural advantages before they fade.

Head Position Controls the Whole Body

  • 1When the head lifts to look forward, the hips immediately drop and the body goes vertical. This is the single most common beginner problem.
  • 2For beginners, we teach them to look down — this keeps the head, neck, spine, and hips in one long horizontal line.
  • 3When swimmers cannot maintain this on their own, we use the Occipital Push — gentle guidance at the base of the skull — to physically teach the correct position until it becomes automatic.

Hips Must Stay Horizontal Too

  • 1Even with the head correctly positioned, the swimmer will sink if the hips drop.
  • 2We use the Hip Grab to support the hips and train them to stay at the surface.
  • 3With repetition and consistency, the hips develop the muscle memory to stay horizontal. Failing to correct the hips will revert all progress.

✓ Horizontal Position

  • Head, spine, hips, legs in one line
  • Buoyancy lifts body to surface
  • Less effort to move forward
  • Kick is effective and propulsive
  • Swimming feels supported

✗ Vertical Position

  • Head up, hips drop, legs sink
  • Water resistance increases
  • Body fights to stay afloat
  • Kick pushes water up and down, not back
  • Swimming feels exhausting and impossible
✓ The AquaCats StandardHorizontal alignment must become muscle memory — in the mind and body. Train it from the first submersion. Correct it every single time it breaks. Never let vertical position become a habit.
02

Momentum

How swimmers learn to move from Point A to Point B

2
Momentum
Continuous forward movement · The goal of every swim · Point A to Point B
What Is Momentum? Momentum is achieved when the swimmer is horizontal AND coordinating their kicks and arms together. It is not just kicking hard — it is the combination of correct body position, consistent kicks, and arm movement all working at the same time. When these three things align, the swimmer moves forward efficiently and naturally stays closer to the surface. Take any one of them away and momentum breaks down.

Why Beginners Need Help With Momentum

  • →Beginners do not yet understand water direction, propulsion, or how their body should move through water. They rely on the instructor to guide them.
  • →By giving forward guidance, the instructor helps the swimmer feel how the body moves through water, understand that swimming has a direction, and experience early success.
  • →Once a swimmer understands that the goal is to move from Point A to Point B, they naturally begin creating their own momentum through kicking and arm movement.

How We Teach It — Blast Off

  • 1The Blast Off method gives the swimmer a controlled forward push — they feel what correct momentum through water feels like.
  • 2This trains the body to move in the correct direction and helps the swimmer understand that swimming is purposeful forward movement.
  • 3Like all AquaCats methods, Blast Off serves multiple purposes — it builds momentum, trains body position, and begins kick coordination all at once.
The Instructor's Role in Momentum Our goal is to help swimmers help themselves. We assist with momentum early so they can feel success — then we progressively reduce that assistance until they are generating their own forward movement. Every method is a stepping stone toward independence, not a permanent crutch.
03

Coordination

How the body works together instead of against itself

3
Coordination
Synchronized movement · Kicks · Arms · Head · Breath · All together
What Is Coordination? Coordination is the ability to synchronize the different parts of the body — kicks, arms, head position, breath, and core — so they work together instead of competing against each other. Water rewards smooth, unified movement and punishes disorganized movement. Coordination is not natural for beginners — it is something we practice and repeat.

Kicking — The Clearest Example

  • ✓Fast, uniform, small kicks: Create steady propulsion and keep legs near the surface. This improves buoyancy and momentum together.
  • ✗Kicks too big or bending knees too much: Pushes water up and down instead of backward — this lifts and drops the body rather than moving it forward.
  • ✗Irregular kicks (slow–fast–stop–start): Breaks momentum and can keep the swimmer stuck in place.
  • ✗Overly splashy kicks: Wastes energy without creating actual propulsion.

What Happens Without Coordination

  • !Kicks and arm movements cancel each other out
  • !The head lifts, causing hips to sink immediately
  • !The body goes vertical instead of horizontal
  • !Momentum disappears — swimming feels exhausting
✓ The Standard — Fast, Small, RhythmicCoordinated kicking for beginners = fast, small, uniform flutter kicks. Train this from the first session and reinforce it every single rep. It is the engine of everything else.
04

Mental Readiness

A relaxed mind leads to a better swimmer — always

4
Mental Readiness & Psychology
Swimming is as much mental as physical · Especially for beginners
Why This Principle Matters Fear, separation anxiety, and uncertainty create physical tension. Physical tension directly reduces buoyancy. A tense child sinks. A relaxed child floats. This is not metaphor — it is physics. Your ability to regulate a swimmer's emotional state is as important as any physical technique you use.

Learning What the Child Responds To

  • 1Every child is different. Some respond to verbal praise. Some respond to toys. Some respond to physical affection — hugs, high fives, gentle encouragement. Find their currency in the first session and use it wisely.
  • 2Crying does not mean not ready. A child's skills and body adjustments determine readiness — not their emotional expression. Crying is normal. Panic, breath refusal, and rigid resistance are different. Know the difference.
  • 3Use rewards strategically — after every successful rep, give them what they respond to. This is how you build cooperation and make the lesson work with you instead of against you.
  • 4Build trust before pushing skills. A swimmer who trusts you will attempt things a swimmer who doesn't trust you will refuse entirely.

The Chain Reaction

Relaxed Mind → Relaxed Body → Better Buoyancy → Better Float → Better Swimming This chain works in reverse too. A tense mind → tense body → lost buoyancy → sinking. Emotional regulation is a safety skill.

Instructor Takeaways

  • 1Use calm, confident, simple cues — your voice is a tool.
  • 2Celebrate every micro-success — "good breath," "great kick," "you did it." Confidence is built rep by rep.
  • 3Know when to push and when not to. Reading this correctly is what makes an AquaCats instructor elite.
  • 4Never show frustration, surprise, or hesitation — your emotional state becomes the swimmer's emotional state.

How All Four Work Together

When one fails, the swimming fails — this is why teaching must be flexible

🌊

Buoyancy & Body Position

Keeps the swimmer afloat and horizontal. The foundation everything else is built on.

💨

Momentum

Moves them from Point A to Point B. Forward motion makes every other principle easier.

🔄

Coordination

Keeps the body working together. Without it, momentum and position both collapse.

🧠

Mental Readiness

Allows the swimmer to relax and cooperate. A tense child loses buoyancy. Period.

⚠️ If One Fails, the Whole System Fails
  • A tense child loses buoyancy → body sinks → position breaks → momentum stops
  • A sinking hip destroys body position → head lifts to compensate → full vertical collapse
  • Interrupted kicks ruin momentum → swimmer stalls → fear increases → tension increases
  • Poor coordination wastes all energy → swimmer tires quickly → gives up

This is why teaching must be flexible — each child needs a different combination of these principles emphasized at different times. Your job is to read which pillar is failing and address that one first.

🔗 How This Connects to Every Method You Will Learn

Every technique in Levels 0–3 maps to one or more of these four principles. When you understand that the Occipital Push fixes body position, the Hip Grab fixes buoyancy, the Blast Off builds momentum, and consistent commands build mental trust — you stop following steps and start coaching. That is the difference.

01

Proximity & Safety Control

The non-negotiable foundation of every lesson

!
Beginner Swimmers Must Always Be Within Arm's Reach
No exceptions · No matter how comfortable they seem
Why This Is Non-Negotiable Poor proximity control is the most dangerous mistake a new instructor can make. The risks happen fast — running from the instructor, jumping unexpectedly, panic responses, and unsafe water entries. By the time you react, it can be too late. Stay close. Always.

How to Maintain Correct Proximity

  • 1Keep the swimmer between your body and the wall when possible — you are the barrier between them and open water.
  • 2Anticipate movement before it happens. Watch their eyes, their weight shift, their body language. Move before they do.
  • 3Never turn your back on a beginner swimmer — not even for a second.
  • 4If a child attempts to leave the step, gently support them and guide them back. Teach them that the step is where they stay — the same way a kindergartner learns to stay at their desk.
🚨 Safety Is Always First Close gates behind you. Pick up toys after use. Be respectful of the fact that you are in someone's home. These are not optional — they are part of the job.
02

The Step as Classroom

Structure exists even in the water

🪜
The Pool Step Is the Swimmer's Entire World
Safety point · Instruction point · Reset space · Reward location
Why the Step Matters The step is not just a place to sit. It is the classroom, the reset zone, the reward location, and the safety point all in one. Teaching swimmers to respect the step teaches them that structure exists in the water — just like it exists everywhere else. This structure is what makes the lesson safe and productive.

Teaching Classroom Etiquette at the Step

  • 1Sit when arriving: Every time a swimmer returns to the step, they sit. Non-negotiable. Teach it from session one.
  • 2Hands on step when waiting: Hands stay on the edge. This prevents unexpected movement and builds the habit of controlled positioning.
  • 3Return to step after every drill: Every swim ends at the step. Every drop ends at the step. The step is always home base.

Behavior Management Through Play

  • →Use toys to encourage sitting and waiting — give them something to do at the step so staying there feels rewarding.
  • →"Clean up" games to restore calm — scatter toys, ask for help picking them up. Redirects attention instantly.
  • →Rhythm activities (counting, singing) to regulate breathing and emotions.
  • →Never force skill work while a swimmer is dysregulated. Use play to regulate first, then work.
03

Coach Voice

A required safety skill — your voice shapes everything

🎙️
Coach Voice Is a Safety Tool
How you sound matters as much as what you say
Why This Matters An instructor's voice directly affects swimmer behavior and emotional state. A hesitant voice creates an uncertain swimmer. A frantic voice creates a scared swimmer. A calm, confident, consistent voice creates a regulated swimmer who trusts you and responds to your cues. Your voice is a tool — use it intentionally.

✓ Coach Voice IS

  • Confident and clear
  • Calm but firm
  • Consistent in tone every lesson
  • Predictable — swimmer knows what to expect
  • Concise — one word commands
  • Animated and energetic
  • Reassuring without being soft

✗ Coach Voice Is NOT

  • Hesitant or unsure
  • Overly loud or frantic
  • Baby-talk
  • Emotionally reactive
  • Frustrated or sharp
  • Monotone or flat
  • Inconsistent lesson to lesson
What a Strong Coach Voice Does Builds trust · Reduces chaos · Prevents unsafe behavior · Improves swimmer response to verbal cues · Creates comfort and predictability. Tone matters as much as the words themselves. A swimmer who hears the same confident voice every session feels safe. A swimmer who feels safe learns faster.

Distraction as a Teaching Tool

  • →Throw toys and ask for help retrieving them — gives the swimmer a job, reduces focus on fear.
  • →Sing or count aloud during drills — rhythm regulates breathing and emotion.
  • →Turn drills into games — "Can you catch the toy?" is more powerful than "Do the drill."
  • →Redirect attention before dysregulation peaks — read the swimmer and intervene early.
✓ The GoalKeep the swimmer's brain focused on play and movement — not on fear. A brain focused on play cannot be fully focused on being scared.

The Parent Relationship

If you have the parent on your side, you are golden

⭐ Why the Parent Relationship Is Everything

Parents know nothing about swimming technique. They cannot evaluate your hip grab or your occipital push. But they will evaluate you on everything they can see — and those things matter enormously. A parent who trusts you gives you freedom to do your job. A parent who doubts you will second-guess every decision you make.

Parents are instructed to watch from inside the house — but they are always watching. Many have cameras too. Conduct yourself accordingly at all times. Your hands must always be visible. Your professionalism must always be on.

👁️
What Parents Actually Judge You On
They can't evaluate technique · But they evaluate everything else
THE PARENT CHECKLIST — What They Are Asking Themselves
😊Are you smiling? Are you warm and approachable?
🤗Are you trying to bond with their child? Do you seem to care?
⏰Are you punctual and reliable? Do you show up when you say you will?
💪Do you appear confident? Do you seem like you know what you're doing?
🎯Do you appear competent? Does the lesson look organized and purposeful?
💬Do you answer questions effectively? Can you explain what you're doing and why?
⚡Are you energetic and animated? Do you bring energy to the lesson?

The Most Important One — Describing Progress Parents may not be able to see progress clearly — especially early on. Your job is to narrate it for them. "Today she held her breath for the first time without any cup pour — that is a huge step." "His kick is getting stronger and more consistent, watch his hips staying higher this week." When you describe progress, parents feel confident in you. And a confident parent is a patient parent.

The Sandwich Technique & Trust

How you communicate with parents and swimmers matters as much as what you communicate

🥪
The Sandwich Technique
Positive → Area to grow → Positive · Always end on strength
What It Is The Sandwich Technique is how you deliver feedback to parents about their child. It keeps communication positive, honest, and constructive — without discouraging the parent or making them defensive. Use it every session when giving a progress update.
🟢Start Positive: Something specific the swimmer did well today. "She held her breath three times in a row — that is real progress."
🟡Middle — Area to Work On: One thing to focus on next. "We are still working on keeping her hips up during the float — that is our focus for next session."
🟢End Positive: Close with confidence and forward momentum. "She is getting more comfortable every single session — you should be proud of how far she's come."
✓ Why It WorksParents leave feeling informed, hopeful, and confident in you. They are not worried — they are excited for next session. That is the relationship you want.
🤝
Trust — With Parents and Swimmers
Once lost, it is hard to rebuild · Protect it in everything you do
Swimmers Lose Trust Fast Children are perceptive. If you tell a swimmer "one more swim" and do three more — they notice. If you promise something and don't deliver — they notice. Trust with a young swimmer is built rep by rep and broken in a single moment. Your word must mean something in the pool, every time.

How to Protect Trust With Swimmers

  • ✓If you say one more swim, do one more swim. Period.
  • ✓Use consistent commands — the same word always means the same thing.
  • ✓Follow through on rewards — if they earn it, they get it.
  • ✓Never surprise them with water contact — 1-2-3 before everything, always.

How to Protect Trust With Parents

  • ✓Be punctual — show up when you say you will, every time.
  • ✓Be transparent — tell them what you are working on and why.
  • ✓Answer questions confidently and honestly — if you don't know, say you'll find out.
  • ✓Always have your hands visible — you are in their home, with their child.
  • ✓Respect their space — close gates, pick up toys, leave the pool area as you found it.

Progress Tracking & EOD Log

Every lesson documented · Every swimmer tracked · No exceptions

📋
End of Day Log — Your Responsibility
Complete after every lesson · Kid level · Achievements · Notes
Why Documentation Matters If a swimmer switches coaches, the new coach needs to know exactly where they are and what has been working. Without documentation, that swimmer loses continuity and progress. The EOD log protects the swimmer, protects you, and keeps AquaCats operating as a professional program — not a personality-based one.

What Goes in the EOD Log

  • 1Swimmer name and current level — confirm which level they are working in that session.
  • 2Skills worked on — what techniques were practiced, how many reps, what the focus was.
  • 3Achievement tracker — note any milestones hit. First independent breath hold, first wall catch without guidance, first full SRS cycle, etc.
  • 4What to focus on next session — so you or any other coach picks up exactly where this session left off.
  • 5Any behavioral or emotional notes — was the swimmer dysregulated? Did a new technique click? Did they regress on something? Document it.

Communicating Progress to Parents

  • →Progress is communicated in person at the end of each lesson using the Sandwich Technique.
  • →Use specific language — not "she did great today" but "she held her breath on cue three times without the cup pour — that is a real breakthrough."
  • →The EOD log backs up what you tell parents. If they ever ask for details, you have them.

Handling Regression

It happens · Know what to do when it does

↩
When a Swimmer Goes Backwards
Go back a couple steps · Rebuild · Never push forward on a broken foundation
Regression Is Normal — How You Handle It Is What Matters A swimmer who was holding their breath consistently and suddenly can't. A Level 1 swimmer whose float collapses. A kid who was regulated and is now refusing to get in. This happens. It does not mean you failed and it does not mean they failed. It means you go back a couple steps, rebuild the foundation, and work forward again.

What To Do

  • 1Identify what broke down — is it physical? Emotional? Did something happen at home? Did they miss several sessions? Read the situation before acting.
  • 2Go back to what they were succeeding at — find the last point of confident success and start from there. Not from where they were last week. From where they are today.
  • 3Rebuild trust and confidence first — regression often comes with emotional resistance. Regulate before you skill-build.
  • 4Never skip steps to "catch up" — pushing a regressed swimmer forward before the foundation is re-established creates bigger problems down the road.
  • 5Document the regression and your response — note it in the EOD log so there is a clear record of what happened and what was done.
How to Explain Regression to Parents Use the Sandwich Technique. "She is doing really well overall and her trust with me is strong. We took a step back on the breath work today because she seemed a little off — that is totally normal and actually shows she is paying attention to her body. We will be right back to where she was by next session." Parents who understand regression don't panic. Your job is to explain it before they have to ask.

Handling Difficult Parents

Try first · Escalate when needed · Always go at the pace they request

👨‍👩‍👧
When Parents Interfere or Push Back
Stay calm · Try first · Escalate to office if needed
Parents Watch Everything — And Sometimes React A parent watching their child cry will sometimes intervene, question your methods, or ask you to stop. This is natural. Your job is to handle it professionally, keep the parent on your side, and escalate only when necessary.

If a Parent Is Watching and Interfering

  • 1First — kindly ask them to go inside. Explain why it helps: "Children often perform better and regulate faster when they can't see mom or dad — it actually helps them bond with me faster. Would you mind watching from inside?" Most parents will respect this if it is framed as being for their child's benefit.
  • 2Always go at the pace the parent requests. If a parent wants a slower, gentler approach — honor that. A slower pace means more bonding time, more play, more trust-building. Adjust your session accordingly and document it.
  • 3Never argue with a parent poolside. If they disagree with something, acknowledge their concern, explain your reasoning calmly, and move on. Confrontation at the pool helps no one.
  • 4If you cannot manage the situation — do not force it. End the lesson professionally and escalate to the AquaCats office. Let the office handle parent conflict. That is what they are there for.
Escalation Is Not Failure Knowing when to escalate is a sign of professionalism, not weakness. If a parent is being unreasonable, aggressive, or is interfering with safety — contact the office. Do not try to manage a serious conflict alone. Document everything and report it the same day.

Professional Conduct & Confidentiality

You are in someone's home with their child · Conduct yourself accordingly

⚖️
Privacy, Confidentiality & Legal Liability
This is serious · Violations can have legal consequences
You Are Legally Responsible for How You Handle Student Information You work in private homes with children. The information you have access to — names, addresses, family details, medical history, images — is private and protected. Mishandling this information is not just unprofessional. It can make you personally and legally liable. Take this seriously.

Videos & Photos

  • 🚫Do NOT take videos or photos of any student without explicit permission from the AquaCats office first. Not for personal use, not for social media, not even to show a parent the technique. Ask the office first. Always.
  • 🚫Do not post any images, videos, or identifying information about students or their families on any social media platform — ever.
  • 🚫Do not share videos of students with other coaches, friends, or family without office approval.

Personal Information

  • 🚫Never share a student's personal information with anyone. Not their name, address, schedule, or family details. Not even casually.
  • 🚫Do not discuss one family's situation with another family — even if you think it is harmless.
  • 🚫Do not use contact information given to you for lesson coordination for any other purpose.

Being in Someone's Home

  • →You are a guest. Treat the property with respect at all times.
  • →Do not enter any part of the home beyond what is necessary for the lesson.
  • →Do not make comments about the home, family, or personal belongings — to them or to anyone else.
  • →Leave the pool area exactly as you found it — gates closed, toys stored, area clean.
The Bottom Line This is a serious job. You are trusted with someone's child and invited into their home. That trust is sacred. Violating confidentiality, sharing images without permission, or mishandling personal information can result in termination and legal consequences. When in doubt — ask the office before you act.

Purpose of Level 0

🎯 What Level 0 Is Building

Level 0 is the swimmer's first introduction to the water environment. Before any survival skill can be taught, the water must feel predictable, safe, and manageable. Your job at this level is not to teach swimming — it is to build the foundation that makes swimming teachable. Every swimmer progresses differently — adjust time and intensity to the individual, not to a fixed schedule.

😢 Understanding Crying vs. Dysregulation

Progression does not require a tear-free swimmer. Kids cry because they are uncomfortable — that is normal and expected. Skills may continue as long as the swimmer is safe, responsive to support, and able to recover.

Keep going when: Swimmer cries but continues responding, clings between skills, or vocalizes discomfort.

Slow down when: Swimmer shows panic, breath refusal, or rigid full-body resistance. This is true dysregulation — not the same as crying.

1
Breath Control — 1-2-3
The most important cue at Level 0 · Used before every single water contact
🚨 This Is the Most Important Thing at Level 0 1-2-3 must be said before every single water contact — every dunk, every cup pour, every submersion. No exceptions, ever. The moment you surprise a swimmer with water, you break trust. Trust is everything at this level. Without the cue, there is no breath control. Without breath control, nothing else works.

Starting Small — Cup Pours

  • 1Start with cup pours on the shoulder — never the face first. Let the swimmer feel water in a non-threatening way.
  • 2Always cue "1… 2… 3…" before water contact. Cue MMM first to prompt mouth closure.
  • 3Stay animated and positive — your energy directly shapes how the swimmer responds.
  • 4Progress to face pours only when the swimmer anticipates the count and closes their mouth.

Progressing to Dunks + Manual Swims

  • 1As the swimmer learns to hold their breath on cue, begin small dunks — very brief at first, a split second. Never hold them under. Build gradually.
  • 2Always cue 1-2-3 before every dunk. As breath control improves, dunks can become slightly longer each session.
  • 3Include manual swims to coach during this block — as they hold their breath, swim them toward you at the step. Short distances, full support. This combines breath control with body position from the start.

Blowing Bubbles

  • 1Have swimmer blow bubbles into the water at the step — playful and low-stakes breath control practice.
  • 2Demonstrate first. Make it fun. Progress to chin, then mouth, then full face.

Timing the Cry

  • 1Watch the swimmer's cry cycle — there is always a brief moment between cries where the mouth closes and a breath is taken.
  • 2Time your dunk to that exact moment of breath intake. Swimmer goes under with air — not mid-cry.

Step Work Sequence

Why This Is Non-Negotiable The step is the classroom, the safety point, and the reset zone. Everything at Level 0 happens at or near the step. A swimmer who can sit, balance, and catch the step is a safer swimmer — and a swimmer who feels safe at the step is a swimmer who can learn.

  • 1Before any step sequence, figure out what this swimmer responds to — verbal praise, hugs, toys, songs. Every child is different. Find their currency early.

  • 1Lower the swimmer to sit on the step with full instructor support.
  • 2Hold until they are balanced and calm — do not rush this.
  • 3Many swimmers resist sitting at first. Stay calm, hold position, and let them adjust. Repetition teaches comfort.

Camina — Walk the Step

  • 1Hold both of the swimmer's hands and walk them side to side along the step.
  • 2Watch for progress over sessions: both hands → one hand → no hands. That progression is pool balance developing — it is a real milestone.
  • 3Camina builds spatial familiarity with the pool edge and develops balance needed for everything that follows.

Step Catch — Superman Hands

  • 1Bring swimmer's arms out in front of them — you will need to physically adjust their arms for them at first. They will not do this naturally.
  • 2Guide their hands to catch the TOP of the step — not the side, not the ledge. The top edge.
  • 3Cue "Superman hands!" every time. After catch: sit up, reward immediately. Consistent reward builds the habit that makes this automatic.
  • 4Begin face-out. Progress to face-in as comfort grows.

Introducing Floating
Shoulder upright → Dip lower → Ears in → U-Method
Start Easy — Even If You Know They Can Do More Always begin at the easiest version of floating, every session, even if the swimmer did well last time. Starting easy builds confidence, reestablishes trust, and lets you assess where they are today before progressing. Never skip steps.

Step 1 — Shoulder Float (Upright Position)

  • 1Place swimmer on your shoulder in an upright position — facing away from you, their body resting against your chest and shoulder.
  • 2Count to 10 out loud. Same count, same calm tone, every session. Build the routine.
  • 3You will probably need to pin them down at first — many swimmers arch back or fight the position. Hold firm, stay calm, keep counting. Repetition teaches relaxation.
  • 4Do not progress until they stop fighting the shoulder position.

Step 2 — Dip Lower Each Session

  • 1Once the swimmer relaxes on the shoulder, begin dipping them slightly lower each session.
  • 2The goal of this phase is to get their ears into the water — that is the marker they are approaching true float position.
  • 3Go slowly. Each dip is a little lower than the last. Cue "Look up" throughout.

Step 3 — U-Method (The Most Important Position)

  • 1Once ears are consistently in the water, shift to the U position — your arms form a U-shape supporting the swimmer's torso, one arm under upper back, one under lower back.
  • 2Head position is critical: You will need to pry their head back — water should be almost going over their forehead and their chin at its highest point. This feels extreme but it is correct. Pry it back a lot.
  • 3Hold in U position and count to 10. This is the position everything is working toward.
  • 4Swimmer's ears should be in the water, body flat, limbs relaxed. Tension means slow down and add more reps at the previous stage.

Common Problems & Fixes

  • !Fighting the position: Hold firm, stay calm, keep counting. Repetition teaches relaxation — not words.
  • !Belly dipping: Increase hip support immediately
  • !Head lifting: Pry it back — gentle but deliberate. Cue "Look up."
  • !Tension/poor buoyancy: Slow down, more reps — tension is the enemy of floating

Lesson Flow

Every session follows this structure — but time spent on each step is your call

🧠 Coach Decision-Making — This Is the Most Important Skill You Have

Every session follows the same five steps in order. What changes is how long you spend on each one. That decision is made by reading the swimmer in front of you — not by a timer or a checklist.

Examples:

  • Swimmer struggles to hold breath → spend more time on breath prep before moving to swims.
  • Advanced or regulated swimmer → move through breath prep quickly, spend more time on body alignment, horizontal swimming, and float work.
  • Swimmer is dysregulated at entry → extend acclimation. Don't start skill work until they're settled.
  • Swimmer floats well but hips keep dropping → add more swim reps focused on body position before returning to float.

There is no fixed ratio. Every lesson is different. Your job is to identify what this swimmer needs most today and give them more of it.

1. Welcome & Acclimation → 2. Breath Prep → 3. Body Alignment → 4. Floating Intro → 5. Drop Test Exposure

02

Drop Test Philosophy

What mastery means and why it matters

💧 What Is a Survival Drop Test?

Drop tests confirm a swimmer's ability to respond independently and safely when entering the water unexpectedly. Mastery means a swimmer can be placed into the water without assistance and successfully:

  • Float to the surface independently, OR
  • Turn, orient themselves, and swim to the nearest safety point (wall or step)

True proficiency is demonstrated when the swimmer can perform this from any angle, in varying conditions, without panic or hesitation. When a swimmer reaches this stage, they are considered swim safe.

🎯 What "Mastery" Really Means

A swimmer has mastered drop tests when they can:

  • Enter the water alone
  • Regain breath control independently
  • Choose a safe action (float or swim)
  • Reach safety without reliance on the instructor

Getting here requires repetition of proven methods, effective body position adjustments, and progressive independence.

Two Pathways — Choose One

Assess the swimmer and select the pathway that fits them best

❓ Why Do We Train Three Different Drop Types?

This is one of the most important things to understand at Level 1. In real life, a child does not choose how they fall into water. They may fall in feet-first, face-first, sideways, or completely tumbled and disoriented — with no warning.

If we only train one type of entry, the swimmer only knows how to recover from one type of entry. That is not water safety — that is a rehearsed trick.

So we train three drop types:

  • Vertical (feet/head first): The most common controlled fall — swimmer enters upright and must find horizontal float.
  • Face-First (horizontal): Swimmer enters face-down — must roll laterally to float. This is also the SRS roll mechanic.
  • Flip (disoriented): Swimmer enters in an unknown position — must figure out which way is up and choose the correct recovery.

Each drop type runs through all five phases (A→E) before moving to the next. A swimmer who can recover from all three entries from any angle is truly swim safe.

🔵 Floating Pathway

  • Vertical drop — Look Up, horizontal float
  • Face-first and/or flip drop
  • Arm tuck + OSH, hips on waterline
  • Swim → Roll → Float → Picked Up
  • Best for babies and buoyant swimmers

🟠 Wall Grab Pathway

  • Toy demo before entry — always
  • Turns and swims to wall independently
  • Choo-Choo to step
  • Elbow → Elbow → Knee → Push out
  • Best for toddlers who instinctively swim
🔒 Required for BOTH Pathways

Regardless of which pathway the swimmer takes, the Safety Test must be passed before advancing to Level 2.

Safety Test: Instructor outside pool, tosses swimmer in → full independent recovery → this is the green light for Level 2. No exceptions.

Drop Test Progression — Phases A through E

Every drop type runs through all five phases before moving to the next type

📌 Within Phase A — Face-Out → Chin Dip → Fully Submerged

Before a swimmer is ready for full submersion drops, Phase A itself has an internal progression. Don't skip steps — each one builds trust and breath control at the swimmer's pace.

  • Step 1 — Face Out of Water: Swimmer enters and recovers with face never submerging. Full instructor control. Focus is on the body response and trust — not breath holding.
  • Step 2 — Chin Dipped: Entry deepens slightly so chin touches the water briefly. Swimmer begins experiencing water on the face in a controlled way. Still full support.
  • Step 3 — Fully Submerged: Full entry with head underwater. Swimmer holds breath through the drop and recovers to float or wall. This is true Phase A mastery before moving to Phase B.

This progression applies to every drop type. Never rush to full submersion — the swimmer's breath control and trust in the process must be solid first.

📋 The Five Phases
Phase Instructor Role Goal
A — Full Support Controls entry and full recovery throughout · Internal progression: face out → chin dip → fully submerged Trust, breath recovery, orientation, alignment
B — Partial Delays contact · child initiates first response · less support only where needed Observing natural instinct
C — Finning No physical contact · moves water underneath swimmer to increase buoyancy True independence with water support
D — Shadow No touching, no finning · remains within arm's reach Calm, controlled supervised independence
E — Independent No intervention of any kind Swim safe standard
🌊 What Is Finning? (Phase C)

Finning is a hands-off support technique where the instructor moves water underneath the swimmer to increase buoyancy and improve body position — without touching the child.

  • Support is applied through water movement, not contact
  • Used only where needed — torso, hips, or a specific limb
  • Reduced and removed as the swimmer stabilizes

Instructor cue: "Support the water, not the swimmer."

🚂 What Is Choo-Choo?

Choo-Choo is the swimmer moving hand-over-hand along the pool wall from wherever they grabbed it back to the step. It is not swimming — it is wall navigation. It bridges the gap between reaching the wall and completing the full survival sequence by exiting the water.

Reaching the wall is not enough. Survival is complete only when the swimmer exits the water. Choo-Choo is what gets them from wall to step so they can exit.

🚂

Hand-over-hand · Wall to step · Both pathways use this

Swim Roll Swim (SRS)

What it is, why we teach it, and how it works at Level 1

🔄 What Is SRS?

Swim Roll Swim teaches swimmers how to move, rest, breathe, and recover independently in the water. Rather than relying on static floating, SRS teaches continuous decision-making — when to swim, when to rest, and how to recover without panic.

SRS mirrors how the body naturally responds to fatigue. Instead of fighting exhaustion, swimmers learn to work with it. The goal is not speed or distance — it is survivability, confidence, and calm under stress.

Rest is taught as an action — not a failure.

💡 Why SRS Appears at Level 1 — and Why It Makes Sense

SRS is a Level 2 skill. So why are we introducing it here?

Because the face-first drop and the SRS roll are the exact same mechanic. Same arm tuck. Same OSH hip rotation. Same waterline position. When a swimmer masters the face-first drop, they are already training the SRS roll — they just don't know it yet.

Once the face-first drop is solid, the coach can name the sequence and introduce the full Swim → Roll → Float pattern. The swimmer is not learning something new — they are connecting something they already know to a name and a purpose.

Think of it this way: the face-first drop teaches the body the mechanic. SRS gives that mechanic a job.

At Level 1, SRS goes only as far as: Swim → Roll → Float → Picked Up. The instructor picks the swimmer up after the float. Rolling back to swim is Level 2. Keep it simple, keep it clean.

  • Face-first drop must be introduced before SRS begins
  • SRS reps and drop reps are always separate blocks — never mix
  • Rolling back to swim = Level 2
SWIM → ROLL → FLOAT 8–10 sec → PICKED UP Level 1 only. No rolling back to swim — that is Level 2.

Lesson Flow

Every session, every time — same structure, coach decides time spent on each block

🧠 The Most Important Rule at Level 1

Isolate your blocks. Manual swim reps and drop reps are never in the same block. SRS reps and drop reps are never in the same block. Each block has one job. Mixing confuses the swimmer and slows progress every time.

How long you spend on each block is your call — read the swimmer. A resistant swimmer needs more time in Phase A before moving forward. A swimmer progressing well moves to the next phase. Calm over distance. Reps over speed.

01

Purpose of Level 2

What changes from Level 1 and why

🎯 What SRS Actually Is — and Why It Matters

SRS is how a swimmer gets from Point A to Point B independently. Simple as that. It is not just a survival tool — it is the foundation of all independent swimming. Without SRS, a swimmer cannot cross the pool on their own. With it, they can go anywhere.

The sequence — swim, roll to rest, roll back, swim again — is how the body manages fatigue in the water. Instead of stopping or panicking when tired, the swimmer has a plan. They roll, breathe, reset, and keep going.

The distance progression at Level 2:

  • Step to instructor: Swimmer does SRS to you. You are close. Focus is clean mechanics — not distance.
  • Short width — wall to wall: You in front first, then step aside. Swimmer goes wall to wall independently.
  • Long width — full pool: Instructor in front for the whole length first, then step aside. Full pool independence with float as recovery.

Level 1 taught swimmers what SRS is. Level 2 teaches them to own it — no contact, no prompting, their decision.

🔄 The Full SRS Cycle — Level 2 Version

At Level 1, the sequence ended with the instructor picking the swimmer up after the float. At Level 2, the swimmer completes the full cycle independently:

SWIM → SELF ROLL → FLOAT 8–12 sec → BACK DOWN → SWIM longer Swimmer self-initiates the roll. No hand needed. No surprise. The command works alone.
⚡ Why Commands Are Critical at Level 2

As instructor hands come off, voice must take over. Commands must produce an automatic response — no hesitation, no waiting for a physical cue.

"One day, no hands will be on them — but the command must still work."

At Level 2, hearing "ROLL" should produce an immediate self-initiated rotation — no touch needed. This is the standard. Build it through repetition and by delaying your hands before every cue.

🧠 The Reinforced Thinking Pattern

This is the real-world survival decision process you are training the swimmer to internalize:

Swim → Roll → Breathe → Decide → Swim
02

The Critical Skill — Body Position During Roll

This is the primary correction focus at Level 2 — understand it fully

📐 What Must Happen — and What Must Not

The most common problem at Level 2 is the swimmer's hips or legs dropping before the roll begins, causing them to go vertical in the water. Once a swimmer goes vertical, the roll cannot execute correctly along the waterline.

The rule: The swimmer must stay aligned with the water surface and execute the roll along the waterline — not sink into vertical first.

✓ Correct

  • Body horizontal at water surface throughout
  • Hips stay up during entire swim phase
  • Roll executes along the waterline
  • Smooth, uninterrupted motion throughout

✗ Incorrect

  • Legs or hips drop before roll begins
  • Body goes vertical during swim phase
  • Rolling too early or too late
  • Gasping or panicking during float recovery
🔧 The Fix

Light hip lift during the swim phase → guide smooth horizontal roll along the waterline → minimal contact throughout. Instructor avoids lifting or carrying the swimmer through transitions — the goal is the least input possible to achieve correct mechanics.

Short distance first · Replace yourself with the wall · Build to full pool width
How Independence Is Built In the beginning, the swimmer swims to you — the instructor is Point B. 01

Purpose of Level 3

What Breathing Up is and why it requires careful teaching

💨 What Is Breathing Up?

Breathing Up is the swimmer's ability to quickly lift their chin out of the water to take a breath — while keeping their body in a horizontal position — and immediately return their face to the water to continue swimming.

It is NOT stopping to breathe. It is NOT floating. It is breathing while moving forward.

This is a fundamentally different skill from anything taught before. It allows the swimmer to keep going instead of always rolling to recover.

SWIM → BREATH UP → SWIM → BREATH UP → ROLL & FLOAT When tired → Roll to float → Calm recovery → Resume swimming. Float is always available.
⚠️ Why This Level Requires Special Care

Because breathing up involves lifting the head, it can easily cause a swimmer's body to shift vertical if it is taught incorrectly or progressed too quickly. Vertical body position reduces safety, endurance, and efficiency.

Breathing up can go wrong when:

  • The head is lifted too high — chin should only break the surface
  • The breath is held too long — quick in, quick back down
  • The kick pauses during the breath — kick never stops, not even for a split second
  • Independence is given too soon — support is earned through progression

For this reason, Level 3 is taught slowly, deliberately, and with hands-on support. The written curriculum gives context and understanding. Exact technique is learned in-person training.

📈 What Is Stamina — and When Does It Come?

Stamina is the ability to sustain physical and mental effort over time. In swimming, stamina is built only after correct breathing mechanics are established. The order is non-negotiable:

  • Breathing mechanics first — chin lift, body position, continuous kick
  • Body position consistency second — horizontal throughout every breath
  • Continuous movement third — no stopping between breaths
  • Stamina fourth — longer distances, more breaths per cycle
  • Distance last — full pool crossing with breathing up

Never build stamina before mechanics are solid. Distance on a broken pattern just makes the problem harder to fix.

    Toy Retrieval — Mastery

    Introduced at Level 2 (step depth) · Mastered at Level 3 · Shallow end to deep end

    🎯 What Toy Retrieval Mastery Means at Level 3

    Swimmers first saw toy retrieval at Level 2 at step depth only. At Level 3, toy retrieval is fully developed and mastered. Swimmers are diving down, catching toys, and progressing from the shallow end of the pool all the way to the deep end.

    The goal remains never depth for its own sake. The goal is body position and kick efficiency. Form drives advancement — when a swimmer has clean head-first alignment and fast controlled kicks, deeper retrieval happens naturally. Depth is the byproduct of correct mechanics, not the target.

    ⚠️ Why Many Swimmers Struggle

    Most swimmers who struggle with toy retrieval are not afraid — they just have bad mechanics. The three most common problems:

    • Kicks too big or too slow: Large slow kicks create drag and prevent descent. Fast small flutter kicks are what propel the swimmer down efficiently.
    • Vertical entry — sinking straight down: Jumping feet-first or dropping straight down is not retrieval. The swimmer must lead with the head, body angled forward, feet trailing last.
    • No body alignment: If the body is not streamlined — head to feet in a line — the swimmer cannot travel efficiently underwater regardless of how hard they kick.

    Fix the position first. The retrieval happens naturally once mechanics are correct.

    Stamina Development (Only After Mechanics Are Correct)
    Never build stamina before breathing mechanics are solid
    GoalSustain calm, controlled effort. Stamina follows mechanics — never the other way.
    • 1Increase swim duration between breaths gradually.
    • 2Increase number of breaths per cycle as control improves.
    • 3Delay float usage — swimmer decides when to roll.
    • 4Track breathing quality and body alignment throughout — not just distance.
    ⚠️ Do Not Rush This Stamina built on broken mechanics is not progress — it is the problem getting worse. Only increase distance and duration when chin, hips, and kick are all correct on every breath.
    6
    Toy Retrieval + Reinforcement + Close
    Position before power · Form drives advancement, not depth
    GoalBuild underwater confidence and alignment. Toy retrieval is position training — not depth training.
    • 1Position before power — body head-first, feet last. Fast small kicks. No vertical jumping.
    • 2Progress: step → shallow water → mid-pool → deeper. Form and calm drive advancement, not depth.
    • 3Limbs must NEVER contact pool floor, walls, steps, or ledges — place toy away from all edges.
    • 4Positive close — end on best rep of the session. Never end on a struggle.
    🎯 Toy Retrieval Depth Progression
    • Step — Minimal depth: Focus on alignment and breath comfort. First introduction only.
    • Shallow water: Increased distance downward. Emphasis on fast, controlled kicks.
    • Mid-pool: Longer underwater travel. Body position becomes critical.
    • Deeper water: Only when alignment and control are consistent. Form drives advancement, not depth.

    Level 4 — Streamline, Side Breathing & Freestyle

    📐
    Streamline — Foundation for Freestyle
    Long horizontal position · Head aligned · Arms extended · Continuous kick
    What Streamline Is Streamline teaches the swimmer to maintain a long horizontal body position — head, spine, and hips aligned — while rotating the head and body together to breathe to the side. This is the foundation all freestyle mechanics are built on. Mastery is required before introducing arm movements.
    • →Maintain long horizontal position — head aligned with spine, hips up
    • →Arms extended forward in stable position — reduce drag
    • →Kick continuously to generate forward movement
    • →Rotate head and body together to the side to breathe — one goggle, one ear in water
    • →Return smoothly to face-down position between breaths
    Side Breathing RuleOne goggle and one ear stay in the water during the breath. Head does not lift — it rotates with the body as one unit.
